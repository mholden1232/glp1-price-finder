How to Appeal a GLP-1 Insurance Denial — and Win

62% of GLP-1 claims were rejected in 2024. Up to 80% of appeals succeed when done correctly. Here's the step-by-step process, plus every ICD-10 code that helps get coverage approved.

Updated March 202615 min read
📊 By the numbers62% of GLP-1 requests were denied in 2024. Up to 80% of well-documented appeals succeed. Only 15% of denied patients ever attempt an appeal — most give up money that should be theirs.

Why You Were Denied

  • Not medically necessary — Insurer claims your condition doesn't warrant it
  • Step therapy required — Must "fail" cheaper drugs first (Contrave, Qsymia, metformin)
  • Plan exclusion — Weight loss drugs excluded from your plan entirely
  • Wrong ICD-10 code — The diagnosis code submitted doesn't match a covered indication
  • Missing prior authorization — PA wasn't submitted or was incomplete

Step-by-Step Appeal Process

  1. Read the denial letter word for word

    Note the exact denial reason and appeal deadline (usually 30–180 days). Missing the deadline forfeits your right to appeal.

  2. Call your doctor immediately

    Your doctor needs to submit a Letter of Medical Necessity (LMN). This is the single most important document in your appeal.

  3. Gather documentation

    A1C results, weight history, sleep study results, cardiac records, documentation of other treatments tried and failed.

  4. Verify your ICD-10 codes

    Ask your doctor to confirm the diagnosis code matches a covered indication. If you have multiple qualifying conditions (obesity + sleep apnea), ensure all codes are submitted.

  5. Submit with complete documentation

    Don't just resubmit the same request. Your appeal must directly address the denial reason with objective clinical evidence.

  6. Request a peer-to-peer review

    Ask your doctor to request a P2P review with the insurer's medical director. This often results in approval when the initial denial was automated.

  7. Escalate to external review

    If your internal appeal is denied, request independent external review. External reviews overturn denials in roughly 40% of cases — and insurers must honor the decision.

What's marketed as "generic" semaglutide is almost always compounded semaglutide — which is not the same thing.

The FDA approved Wegovy HD at 7.2mg, delivering 20.7% average weight loss in clinical trials — comparable to tirzepatide. A US launch in a single-dose pen is expected April 2026, approved under the FDA's new National Priority Voucher accelerated review program.

India gets real generics — the US does not

Semaglutide's patent expired in India on March 20, 2026. Indian manufacturers launched generic versions priced roughly 90% below brand-name. Canada is also expected to see generic semaglutide later in 2026. These generics are not legally available in the United States.

The honest answer: not for several years. Novo Nordisk's overlapping patents on semaglutide are expected to protect the molecule in the US through 2031–2032. A meaningful price drop from an FDA-approved biosimilar or generic is unlikely before 2033–2036.

Why HRT and GLP-1s Work Better Together — Especially After 40

A landmark 2026 Mayo Clinic study found women on both therapies lost 35% more weight than those on GLP-1 alone. Here is what the science says and what it means for you.

Updated April 202610 min read
🌸 Key findingA January 2026 Mayo Clinic study published in The Lancet Obstetrics, Gynaecology & Women's Health found that postmenopausal women on both HRT and tirzepatide lost approximately 35% more weight than women taking tirzepatide alone.

Why Menopause Makes Weight Loss Harder

If you are in your 40s or 50s and finding that the diet and exercise routine that worked for years suddenly stopped working — you are not imagining it. The hormonal changes of perimenopause and menopause fundamentally alter how your body processes and stores fat.

As estrogen declines, fat storage shifts from the hips and thighs toward the abdomen. Insulin sensitivity worsens. Muscle mass decreases. Metabolism slows. These are biological changes, not lifestyle failures.

This is why many women in midlife struggle with weight gain even when they are doing everything right — and why GLP-1 medications alone may not deliver the same results for postmenopausal women as they do for younger patients.

How GLP-1s and HRT Work Together

GLP-1 receptor agonists like semaglutide and tirzepatide work by mimicking a natural gut hormone that signals fullness to the brain, slows digestion, and improves insulin sensitivity. They are powerful tools for weight loss — but their effectiveness depends in part on your hormonal environment.

Here is where HRT comes in. Estrogen actually upregulates GLP-1 receptors in the gut and brain. More receptors means a stronger signal from the same dose of medication. Estrogen also stimulates your intestinal cells to produce more of your own natural GLP-1 — so you start from a higher baseline.

When estrogen drops in menopause, that entire system becomes less efficient. You may need higher doses to get the same effect, or simply not respond as well. Adding HRT can essentially restore the hormonal conditions that allow GLP-1 medications to work the way they did in clinical trials — which were conducted largely in younger, premenopausal women.

What the Research Shows

Mayo Clinic / The Lancet (January 2026)

Researchers reviewed data from 120 postmenopausal participants with overweight or obesity who received tirzepatide for 12 or more months. Women using both tirzepatide and HRT lost approximately 35% more weight than those taking tirzepatide alone. The research team noted "a potential synergy, with estrogen appearing to enhance the appetite-suppressing effects of GLP-1."

Menopause Journal (2024)

A study of postmenopausal women on semaglutide found greater total body weight loss at every checkpoint over 12 months among women who were also using hormone therapy compared to those who were not — with a 16% versus 12% loss at the 12-month mark.

Nature Medicine

Research noted that estrogen and GLP-1 may work together to influence areas of the brain that control eating behavior and energy use — suggesting the synergy operates at a neurological level, not just metabolic.

⚠️ Important caveatThese studies are observational, not randomized controlled trials. Randomized trials are currently underway. The evidence is clinically meaningful but not yet definitive. HRT is not appropriate for everyone — women with a history of certain cancers, blood clots, or other conditions may not be candidates. Always discuss with a licensed provider.

Who This Combination May Help Most

  • Women in perimenopause who are starting to notice weight gain despite no change in habits
  • Postmenopausal women on a GLP-1 who are not seeing the results they expected
  • Women with menopausal symptoms like hot flashes, night sweats, and sleep disruption — which independently worsen weight management
  • Women with significant abdominal fat gain since entering menopause, even without major weight change overall

What to Ask Your Doctor

If you are a perimenopausal or postmenopausal woman considering a GLP-1, it is worth having a conversation with your provider about your hormonal status before starting. Ask whether checking estrogen, progesterone, testosterone, LH, and FSH before beginning a GLP-1 makes sense for your situation. Optimizing hormones may be what makes the treatment work as intended.

If you are already on a GLP-1 and not getting the results you expected, bring the Mayo Clinic study to your next appointment. It is a legitimate clinical question worth discussing.

Where to Find Combined GLP-1 + HRT Care

Most traditional GLP-1 telehealth providers do not offer HRT alongside their weight loss programs. But a growing number of platforms are beginning to offer both — recognizing that for women in midlife, addressing hormonal and metabolic health together produces better outcomes.
